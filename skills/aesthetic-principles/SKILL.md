# Aesthetic Principles

## Core Truth: Restraint Signals Quality

The single most important difference between amateur and professional visual work:
- Amateurs: more colors, more effects, more detail, more everything
- Professionals: fewer things, each chosen with more care

**Every element you add dilutes the impact of every other element.**

Rule of thumb: If you cannot explain why each element is there, remove it.

## The Iron Rule

**Design with the 20-mile march in mind** (the discipline of steady, deliberate progress — resist scope creep and arbitrary additions).

Resist adding elements just because they are possible. Every design decision should earn its place. If it does not serve the user's understanding or the emotional goal, it is noise.

## What Makes Work Feel "Chosen, Not Generated"

A viewer should feel that a human with taste made deliberate choices:
- Typography feels right before you read a word
- Whitespace makes content feel curated, not crammed
- Images feel chosen, not stock
- Navigation feels obvious without being obvious
- Everything unnecessary has been removed

## Universal Anti-Patterns

These scream "made without taste or judgment":

1. **Rainbow palettes** — multiple saturated colors without deliberate hierarchy
2. **Gradient backgrounds on everything** — gradients as a substitute for not knowing how to create depth
3. **Default dark background with neon accents** — the cliched "AI art" look
4. **No dominant color** — equal visual weight everywhere means nothing stands out
5. **Competing CTAs** — multiple elements all demanding attention simultaneously
6. **Gradients as depth substitute** — not knowing how to create spatial hierarchy so reaching for a gradient

## Typography Hierarchy

**Typography should be visible as hierarchy before you read a single word.**

When someone lands on a page, they should instinctively understand:
- What is the main headline
- What is secondary
- What is navigational
- What they should read first

If the typography does not communicate this before reading, the hierarchy is broken.

## Whitespace Is Not Empty Space

Whitespace gives the eye a place to rest and signals that the content is curated, not crammed. More whitespace almost always reads as more premium. The instinct to fill space is almost always wrong.

## The Resonance Note (Meta-Advisory)

Humans learn what is good from copying and studying what other humans really like. If something is popular and resonates widely, it might not match your personal aesthetic — but the resonance itself is information. That resonance tells you something landed. Use that data. Study what works on people, not just what works on you.

This does not mean copy what is popular. It means: pay attention to why things resonate. The gap between "I would have done it differently" and "this works on millions of people" contains a lesson.

## Design Direction Principles

When choosing a visual direction for a project:
- Match the design to the content and audience
- Ask: what feeling should this convey before any words are read?
- Reference sites that achieve similar emotional goals — study them completely
- Define anti-patterns explicitly — what to explicitly NOT do
